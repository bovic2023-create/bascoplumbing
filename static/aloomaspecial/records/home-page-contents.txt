Bathroom Plumbing Repair: Component-Level Diagnosis for Toilet, Shower, and Sink<h2>
A running toilet wastes up to 200 gallons of water per day according to water utility estimates, but determining why it runs requires understanding which of three components is involved. The fill valve refills the tank after each flush and shuts off when the float rises to the set water level. The flapper is the rubber seal at the base of the tank that closes after the flush to hold the water for the next cycle. The flush valve seat is the rim the flapper presses against to form its seal.
The dye test separates these: add food coloring to the tank without flushing, wait 15 minutes. If color appears in the bowl, water is moving past the flapper, meaning the flapper has failed or the flush valve seat has mineral deposits or corrosion preventing a complete seal. If no color appears but the toilet still runs, the fill valve is not shutting off, or the float is set so high that water spills continuously into the overflow tube.
Wax ring failure produces a different and more urgent symptom: water appearing at the floor around the toilet base, intermittent sewage odor at floor level, or a toilet that rocks when sat on. The wax ring creates the watertight seal between the toilet's discharge horn and the drain flange in the floor. A failed wax ring requires lifting the toilet off the floor, cleaning the old wax, and seating a new ring before resetting the toilet.
Shower systems present their own diagnostic category. A single-handle shower valve uses a cartridge that modulates hot and cold flow together. When the cartridge wears, temperature control becomes imprecise, the valve drips when in the closed position, or the handle requires unusual force to operate. Pressure-balancing valves, required by current plumbing codes in showers and tub-shower combinations, maintain a fixed ratio of hot and cold regardless of supply pressure changes elsewhere in the home. When a pressure-balancing valve fails, the shower responds dramatically to any other fixture use: flushing the toilet briefly drops cold supply pressure and shifts the shower toward scalding until the pressure equalizes.

Emergency Plumbing: The 90-Second Window That Determines the Damage Scope<h2>
When a pipe bursts, the single most important action in the first 90 seconds is finding and closing the main water shutoff valve. The damage difference between 90 seconds of uncontrolled water and 10 minutes of uncontrolled water can be measured in thousands of dollars of flooring, drywall, and structural repair.
Most homes have one of two types of main shutoff valve. A ball valve uses a lever handle on a sphere with a hole drilled through it. When the lever runs parallel to the pipe, the valve is open. Turn the lever to perpendicular and the valve closes in a single quarter-turn. Ball valves are reliable even after years without use. A gate valve uses a wheel handle on a wedge-shaped gate mechanism. Multiple clockwise rotations are required to fully close it. In homes built before approximately 1970, gate valves are common and can seize after years without exercise, making them difficult or impossible to close in an emergency. If the main valve fails, individual fixture shutoffs, called angle stop valves, control supply to specific fixtures: one behind the toilet near the floor, two under each sink.

Slab Leak Detection: What Changes When the Pipe Material Changes<h2>
Slab leaks in copper pipe, galvanized steel pipe, and polybutylene pipe behave differently, develop differently, and are assessed differently because the failure mode of each material is specific.
Copper pipe fails primarily at fittings and elbows from electrolytic corrosion, often producing a small, sustained water loss that affects the bill for weeks before becoming visible. Galvanized steel pipe corrodes from the inside out: the interior surface builds up with rust and mineral deposits over decades, narrowing the effective flow diameter before any leak develops. The first signs in a galvanized system are often reduced flow at fixtures and rust-colored water from the hot water side rather than a water-loss event. When galvanized pipe eventually fails under a slab, rust staining frequently appears before water reaches the surface. Polybutylene pipe fails at its fittings specifically, due to degradation from chlorinated water exposure.
In all cases, the concrete access process requires care, and in post-tension slabs, it requires verification of cable locations before any saw or jackhammer work begins

Drain Cleaning and Hydrojetting: The Chemistry Behind Recurring Grease Blockages<h2>
Kitchen drain lines that block repeatedly at the same location are not experiencing random accumulation. They are experiencing progressive saponification: a chemical process in which the alkaline conditions of a drain pipe, produced by soap and cleaning products, partially convert fatty acid deposits from cooking grease into soap-like compounds that adhere to pipe walls more strongly than simple solidified grease. These saponified deposits require high-pressure mechanical removal to dislodge. Hot water alone only re-liquefies the surface layer while leaving the underlying deposits intact.
Floor drain maintenance is a related service that most homeowners have never considered: floor drains in utility rooms, garages, and laundry rooms have P-traps that must hold water to block sewer gas from entering the space. In drains that are used infrequently, the trap water evaporates, allowing hydrogen sulfide odor to enter the space. The solution is periodic: pour a gallon of water into the floor drain to restore the trap seal.
